Real Exchange Rate Adjustment vs 'Standard' Inflation (Arnold C. Harberger 2010)


Perhaps the most common point of misunderstanding is the confusion of the real exchange rate with the nominal rate. One manifestation of this is the erroneous notion that real exchange rate (RER) adjustment can take place only by way of movements in the nominal rate. That is, people seem reluctant to recognize that RER adjustment can take place quite naturally and normally, even when a country has a fixed exchange rate.

‘Standard’ Inflation (Type A) Syndrome
a) loss of international reserves
b) depreciation of the currency
c) expectation of further depreciation
d) people reduce their holdings of monetary balance (M/P) in real terms.

Real Exchage Rate Adjustment (Type B) Inflation Syndrome
a) international reserves increase
b) currency appreciations in real terms
c) no flight from the currency (i.e., no expectation of nominal depreciation)
d) people increase their holdings of real monetary balances (M/P)

In standard macroeconomic analysis, it is very widely accepted that it is wise to avoid, or at least to strenuously combat, strong deflationary pressures. This is an almost sacred tenet of the Keynesian and neo-Keynesian literature, but it is also strongly held by most modern monetarists. Milton Friedmen in particular blamed most of the suffering of the Great Depression on the fact that the Federal Reserve permitted the nominal money supply of the United States to undergo a major decline, which in turn led to strong deflationary pressures during the early years of the depression.

The point of agreement among all these schools is that there exist a certain downward rigidity of wages and of certain important components of the price level. As a result of this rigidity (or very slow adjustment), deflationary pressures (downward shifts of demand for goods and services), are reflected only partly in reduced nominal prices and/or wages. The other part consists of a fall in aggregate output and employment. To aim at keeping total aggregate demand from falling has thus been a hallmark of economists’ advice concerning macroeconomic policy in situations of actual or potential recession.

The alternative scenario in which the price level was kept constant would have entailed the nominal exchange rate E falling from 28 in 2000 to something less than 9 in 2010. With world tradables prices rising by five-eights, the internal price level of tradables would have to fall from an initial level of 100 to around 50 (=1.63/3.39). Obviously, this would mean an incredibly massive deflation of the internal price level of tradables. Subsectors of tradables in which there were substantial amounts of economic rent and the underlying supply curves were quite inelastic (agriculture and mining) might be able to handle this without generating large amounts of unemployment. But it is extremely difficult (I would say almost impossible) to imagine Russian manufacturing being able to absorb such a substantial deflationary pressure without generating massive unemployment in the process.
