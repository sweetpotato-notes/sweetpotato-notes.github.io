Group


Viewed times

In mathematics, a group is an algebraic structure consisting of a set of elements equipped with an operation that combines any two elements to form a third element and that satisfies four conditions called the group axioms, namely closure, associativity, identity and invertibility.

A group is a set, G, together with an operation • (called the group law of G) that combines any two elements a and b to form another element, denoted a • b or ab. To qualify as a group, the set and operation, (G, •), must satisfy four requirements known as the group axioms:
Closure
For all a, b in G, the result of the operation, a • b, is also in G.
Associativity
For all a, b and c in G, (a • b) • c = a • (b • c).
Identity element
There exists an element e in G such that, for every element a in G, the equation e • a = a • e = a holds. Such an element is unique (see below), and thus one speaks of the identity element.
Inverse element
For each a in G, there exists an element b in G, commonly denoted a−1 (or −a, if the operation is denoted “+”), such that a • b = b • a = e, where e is the identity element.

The product set S×T is the set of all ordered pairs (s,t) of elments s∈S and t∈T.

A general relation between S and T is any subset of S×T.

A subset R of S×S is called an equivalance relation on S if:

1) reflexivity, sRs always holds

2) symmetry, sRt implies tRs

3) transitivity, sRt and tRu imply sRu

A partition of S is a representation of S as the union of nonempty, mutually disjoint subsets of S.

A mapping, or function, from S into T is a subset R of S×T such that for any s∈S, there is exactly one pair in R having s as its first member.

Two algebraic systems S and T are called isomorphic if there is a 1-1 correspondence between the elements, relations, and operations of S and T such that under the correspondence of elements all relations and operations are preserved.

If we have the structures S and T such that for each relation and operation in S there is a corresponding relation or operation in T (but not necessarily conversely), and we have a mapping α from S into T which preserves the relations and operations of S, then we say that α is a homomorphism of S into T.

A (multiplicative) group G is cyclic if there is an element a∈G such that for any b∈G there is some integer j (positive, negative, or zero) such that b=aj such an element a is called a generator of the cyclic group, and we write G=(a)

A permutation, π, of the set S is a 1-1 mapping of S onto S.

Any permutation π of a set of n symbols can be expressed as a product of disjoint cycles (i.e. cycles no two of which alter any common symbol).

The order of a group G is the number n of elements in the group, if this number is finite. Otherwise, G is said to be of infinite order.

(Cayley) Every group G is isomorphic to a permutation group of its own elements.

The set of all automorphisms of a group G is a group.

A nonempty subset H of the group G is a subgroup of G iff ab−1∈H for all a,b ∈H

If Xπ=X, then π is an even permutation, if Xπ=-X, then is an odd permutation.

If H is a subgroup of G, then H defines a partition of G (and an equivalence relation on the set G).

If H is a subgroup of order m in a group G of order n, then m divides n, and every coset aH, or Ha, has the same number of elements as H.

The number of cosets of the subgroup H in G (including the identity coset eH=He=H) is the index of H in G, written [G:H].

(Theorem of Lagrange) The order of a finite group G is the product of the order of any subgroup H and the index of H in G.

The subgroup H of G is normal in G if aha−1∈H for all a∈G and all h∈H (or, equivalently, if b−1hb∈H for all b∈G, h∈H). The group G is simple if its only normal subgroups are G itself and the identity subgroup E=(e).

For any subgroup H of G and any element a of G, the set aHa−1={aha−1:h∈H} is a subgroup of G isomorphic to H, H≈aHa−1

For H a subgroup of G and a∈G, aHa−1 is called a conjugate subgroup of H; the isomorphic mapping of H, h→aha−1, is called conjugation by a; and the elements h and aha−1 are said to be conjugate.

The subgroup H of G is normal iff H is equal to all its conjugates (i.e. H is self-conjugate) and iff H is invariant (as a group, not elementwise) under all the inner automorphisms of G.

The subgroup H of G is normal in G iff the left cost aH is also the right coset Ha for every a∈G.

If H is a normal subgroup of G, G/H will denote the set of all cosets of H.

For subgroups H and K of G, the set HK is a subgroup of G iff HK=KH. This condition is satisfied if either H or K is normal in G, and if both H and K are normal in G, then so is HK.

If K is a normal subgroup of G, then G/K is also a group with respect to the operation (aK)(bK)=(ab)K

The kernel of the homomorphism a:G→H of a group G into the group H is the set

K={g:g∈G, ga=ea=e’, the identity of H}.

Thus the kernel of a homomorphism is the set of elements having the identity element as image.

If θ contains all automorphisms of G, then a composition series is called a characteristic series, and if θ is the set of all endomorphisms of G, then a composition series is called a fully invariant series.

(Schreier Refinement Theorem) Two given subinvariant series of the θ-group G have refinements which are isomorphic.

(Jordan-Holder theorem) Any two composition series of an θ-group G are isomorphic.

Every finite θ-group G has a composition series.

If G is an Abelian group and T its torsion subgroup, then G/T is torsion-free.

An Abelian torsion group G is isomorphic to the weak direct product of its Sylow p-subgroups.

If the order of a finite group G is divisible by a prime p, then G contains an element of order p.

Every finite p-group has a composition series in which the factor groups are Abelian.
