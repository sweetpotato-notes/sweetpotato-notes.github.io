Abstract Algebra (P. B. Garrett)


Viewed times

Theorem (Fermat): Let p be a prime number. Then for any integer x, xp ≡ x (mod p)

A subset H of a group G is said to be a subgroup if, with the same operation and identity element as that used in G, it is a group.

The order of a finite group is the number of elements in it, sometimes written as |G|.

Theorem (Lagrange): Let G be a finite group. Let H be a subgroup of G. Then the order of H divides the order of G.

The index [G : H] of a subgroup H in a group G is the number of disjoint (left or right) cosets of H in G. Thus, Lagrange’s theorem says |G| = [G: H]|H|
Proposition: A homomorphic image of a finite cyclic group is finite cyclic.
Let G be a group and H a subgroup. The quotient set G/H of G by H is the set of H-cosets G/H = {xH: x∈G}.
An isomorphism of a group to itself is an automorphism.
A ring R is a set with two operations, + and , and with a special element 0 (additive identity) with most of the usual properties we expect or demand of addition and multiplication:
R with its addition and with 0 is an abelian group.
The multiplication is associative
The multiplication and addition have left and right distributive properties.
A commutative ring in which every nonzero element is a unit is a field.
The kernel of a ring homomorphism f: R→S is
ker f = {r∈R: f(r) = 0}
An ideal in R is an additive subgroup I of R such that R*I ⊆ I. That is, I is an R-submodule of R with (left) multiplication.

Proposition: For a commutative ring R with uint, and for an ideal I, the quotient ring R/I is a field if and only if I is a maximal ideal.

A linearly independent set of vectors spanning a subspace W of V is a basis for W.

A field K is algebraically closed if every non-constant polynomial f(x)∈k[x] has at least one root α∈k, that is, f(α)=0
Upon division, this algebraic closure property implies that any polynomial in K[x] factors into linear factors in K[x].

Theorem: Any field k has an algebraic closure k, unique up to isomorphism. Any algebraic field extension E of k has at least one injection to k (which restricts to the identity on k).

Proposition: Let (non-constant) M(x) be an irreducible in k[x], with field k. Let I be the ideal generated in k[x] by M(x). Let α be the image of x in the field K = k[x]/I. Then α is a root of the equation M(x) = 0

Proposition: Let P(x) be a polynomial in k[x] for a field k. The equation P(x)=0 has a root α generating a degree d extension K of k if and only if P(x) has a degree d irreducible factor f(x) in k[x].

Corollary: The polynomial xn-1 has no repeated factors in k[x] if the field k has characteristic not dividing n.

Theorem: Let G be a finite subgroup of k* for a field k. Then G is cyclic.

Corollary: For a finite field k, the multiplicative group k* is cyclic.

Given ground field Fq and α in an algebraic extension E of Fq, the images α, αq, αq2… of α under the Frobenius are the (Galois) conjugates of α over Fq.

Proposition: Inside a given algebraic extension E of Fq, the property of being conjugate is an equivalence relation.

Corrollary: Given α in an algebraic field extension E of Fq, the number of distinct conjugates of α over Fq is equal to the degree [Fq(α):Fq]

Two n-by-n matrices A and B with entries in a field k are conjugate if there is an invertible n-by-n matrix g with entries in k such that B = gAg-1
Conjugacy is obviously an equivalence relation. The conjugacy classes of n-by-n matrices with entries in k are the corresponding equivalence classes with respect to this equivalence relation.

Theorem (Gauss): Let R be a unique factorization domain. Then the polynomial ring in one variable R[x] is a unique factorization domain.

Corollary: A polynomial ring k[x1, … xn] in a finite number of variables x1, … xn over a field k is a unique factorization domain. (Proof by induction.)

The field of fractions k of an integral domain R is the collection of fractions a/b with a,b∈R and b≠0 and with the usual rules for addition and multiplication.

Theorem: Every permutation is uniquely expressible as a product of disjoint cycles.

Naive definition: A set is an unordered collection of things (not counting multiplicities), its elements.

Corollary: The only order-preserving bijection of a well-ordered set X to itself is the identity map.

A set S is Dedekind infinite if there is an injection from S to a proper subset of S. It is Dedekind finite if it is not Dedekind infinite.

Theorem: Let G be a finite subgroup of the multiplicative group kof a field k. Then G is cyclic.
A finite (hence, algebraic) field extension K of k is seperable if the number of (nonzero) field maps σ: K→k of K to an algebraic closure k* of k is equal to the degree [K:k]

A finite field extension K of k is normal if all k-algebra homomorphisms of K into a fixed algebraic closure k* of k have the same image.

A finite field extension K of k is Galois if it is both separable and normal over k. Let K be a finite Galois field extension of k. The Galois group of K over k is the automorphism group G = Gal(K/k) = Aut(K/k)
The Galois group of a polynomial f in k[x] over k is the Galois group of the splitting field of f over k.

Theorem: The general nth degree polynomial equation is not solvable in terms of radicals for n>4.
