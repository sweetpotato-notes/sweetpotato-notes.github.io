Hooke’s law provides one of the most frequently encountered examples of a nonconstant force.
M[d2x/dt2] = -kx

Rotational invariance and vectors

x’ = xcosθ + ysinθ

y’ = -xsinθ + ycosθ

Vector Algebra

ex · ex = 1

ex · ey = 0

ey · ex = 0

ex × ex = 0

ex × ey = 1

ey × ex =-1

a · (b × c) = b · (c × a) = c · (a × b)

a × (b × c) = (a · c)b - (a · b)c

The gradient of the scalar, grad φ or ▼φ is defined as the vector:

▼φ=(dφ/dx) dx + (dφ/dy) dy + (dφ/dz) * dz

The divergence can be expressed symbolically as:

▼· A= dAx/dx + dAy/dy + dAz/dz

The vector product of the del (▼) operator with a vector is called the curl of the vector and is itself a vector:

▼×A = (dAz/dy - dAy/dz) ex + (dAz/dy - dAy/dz) ey (dAz/dy - dAy/dz) ez
▼×(▼×A ) = ▼(▼· A ) - ▼2A

▼×(▼φ ) = 0

▼·(▼×φ ) = 0

Harmonic oscillator

x = Aei(wt-δ)

Fourier transform

x(u) = ∫X(v)e-iuvdv
X(v) = (1/2π)∫x(u)eiuvdu

F(t) = ∫f(w)e-iwtdw
f(w) = (1/2π)∫F(t)eiwtdt

The Lagrangian of a system is defined as the difference between its kinetic and its potential energies:
L = T - U
d[dL/dv]/dt - dL/dx = 0

The laws of physics are invariant with respect to transformations between inertial systems.

Maxwell’s equations imply that the time and space variations of the electric and magnetic field vectors in a vacuum are given by solutions to the partial differential equations:
▼ · E = 0
▼ · H = 0
▼× E =-(1/C)dH/dt
▼× H = (1/C)dE/dt
▼2E - d2E / dt2 = 0

▼2H - d2H / dt2 = 0

The absolute temperature, pressure, and volume occupied by an ideal gas are related by an empirically derived relation of a particularly simple form:

PV = nRT

Here n, the mass of the gas occupying the volume V, is expressed in moles, where for a compound (or element) with molecular (atomic) weight A, 1 mole = A g, and R is the universal gas constant.
